The Lack Of Power of Retirement Accounts

April 1st, 2024
Last Updated: April 20th, 2024

What is a Retirement Account?

Retirement accounts in the United States are similar (and often look the exact same) to a regular brokerage account. In these accounts, one can buy public stocks, bonds, annuities, and other financial instruments. The sole purpose of these accounts is the exact same as a brokerage (accumulating/preserving wealth for future consumption), but with extra benefits and limitations. The IRS has defined various retirement accounts has having different tax treatments than a regular brokerage account.

Here is a list of some common retirement accounts:

  • [Traditional/Roth/Simple] 401K
  • [Traditional/Roth/Simple] IRA
  • Simplified Employee Pension (SEP)
The IRS themselves give a nice list of available retirement accounts: Link.

Why Use a Retirement Account over a Taxable Brokerage Account?

If your goals require income in your retirement years, then using a retirement account will likely yield more after-tax wealth to spend than just using a taxable brokerage account alone. I will go over the various benefits and limitations of common retirement accounts, and then will share a strategy that I employ that may surprise you to the (lack of) power of these accounts (Skip Ahead).

The different kinds of retirement accounts usually fall into two kinds of buckets

  1. Tax-deferred
  2. Tax-free
Tax-deferred accounts allow you to reduce taxes today, and when it comes time to withdraw, you pay whatever the income tax is at that time on those withdrawals. Tax-free accounts allow you to contribute after-tax dollars today, while promising that any withdrawals in the future will not be taxed.

The Traditional 401K

A tradtional 401K is known as a tax-deferred account. This type of account is obtained through an employer. Most employers have a 401K plan as a benefit to employees. If your employer does not offer this kind of plan, you still may be able to use an IRA instead.

The Benefits

  • Contributions reduce your current Adjusted Gross Income (AGI), which limits the amount of your income that is subjected to federal and state income taxes.
  • Any interest, dividends, or other income generated from investments in the account are shielded from all taxes while they remain in the account.
  • Employers often give what is called a "match". This is often a one-for-one dollar match of your contributions, up to a certain percentage. This is similar to getting double your money up to that match.

The Limitations

  • In 2024, personal annual contribution limitations were $23,000. Contributions that you put in and what your employeer puts in together caps at $69,000. If you're 50 years or older, you are given an extra $7,500 to personally contribute, increasing your limit to $30,500.
  • Withdrawals from this account are subject to current income taxes.
  • Withdrawing before the age of 59½ also tacks on a 10% early-withdraw penalty.
  • The Traditional 401K is subject to Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs). These are age-based rules that force you to withdraw from the account every year once you hit a certain age. This can force you to withdraw more than what you need, making you pay more taxes than you wanted to in a given year.

The Traditional IRA

A Traditional IRA (Individual Retirement Account) is known as a tax-deferred account. This type of account is available to everyone, given they meet certain requirements. This plan is often used by those who do not have a 401K available from their employer.

The Benefits

  • Contributions reduce your current Adjusted Gross Income (AGI), which limits the amount of your income that is subjected to federal and state income taxes.
  • Any interest, dividends, or other income generated from investments in the account are shielded from all taxes while they remain in the account.
  • 401K balances can be "rolled over", or transfered, to an IRA. These transfers are not subject to income or contribution limitations, nor do they incur any penalities or taxes.

The Limitations

  • In 2024, personal annual contribution limitations were $7,000. If you're 50 years or older, you are given an extra $1,000 to personally contribute, increasing your limit to $8,000.
  • You may only contribute wage-based income to your IRA. For example, if you only earned $4,000 in wages for a given year, then you can only contribute $4,000 dollars, even if the limit is $7,000. Income from other sources like dividends are not allowed to be used as contributions.
  • If you or your spouse are already participating in a 401K plan, the amount of contributions that you can use to deduct from your AGI is reduced. Rules for reduced AGI deductions can be researched more at the IRS.
  • Withdrawals from this account are subject to current income taxes.
  • Withdrawing before the age of 59½ also tacks on a 10% early-withdraw penalty.
  • The Traditional IRA is subject to Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs). These are age-based rules that force you to withdraw from the account every year once you hit a certain age. This can force you to withdraw more than what you need, making you pay more taxes than you wanted to in a given year.

The Roth 401K

A Roth 401K is known as a tax-free account. This type of account is obtained through an employer. Although most employers will have a Traditional 401K plan, a Roth 401K is often more rare.

The Benefits

  • Gains are completely tax-free. Contributions can be withdrawn at any time, with no penalities or taxes.
  • Withdrawing from this account does not increase your AGI. This can be used in retirement to lower your AGI to receive extra benefits.
  • Contribution maximum is equal to that of a Traditional 401K, which makes it much more powerful than a Roth IRA.
  • No RMDs.
  • No income limits.

The Limitations

  • In 2024, personal annual contribution limitations were $23,000. Contributions that you put in and what your employeer puts in together caps at $69,000. If you're 50 years or older, you are given an extra $7,500 to personally contribute, increasing your limit to $30,500. Contribution maximums are shared between a Traditional 401K and a Roth 401K. That means that you can only contribute up to $23,000 between the account types per year.
  • Withdrawing before the age of 59½ tacks on a 10% early-withdraw penalty.

The Roth IRA

A Roth IRA is known as a tax-free account. This type of account is available to everyone, given they meet certain requirements.

The Benefits

  • Gains are completely tax-free. Contributions can be withdrawn at any time, with no penalities or taxes.
  • Withdrawing from this account does not increase your AGI. This can be used in retirement to lower your AGI to receive extra benefits.
  • No RMDs.

The Limitations

  • In 2024, personal annual contribution limitations were $7,000. If you're 50 years or older, you are given an extra $1,000 to personally contribute, increasing your limit to $8,000. Contribution maximums are shared between a Traditional IRA and a Roth IRA. That means that you can only contribute up to $7,000 between the account types per year.
  • You may only contribute wage-based income to your Roth IRA. For example, if you only earned $4,000 in wages for a given year, then you can only contribute $4,000 dollars, even if the limit is $7,000. Income from other sources like dividends are not allowed to be used as contributions.
  • There are income limitations to contribute to a Roth IRA. The IRS provides a page on this.
  • Withdrawing before the age of 59½ tacks on a 10% early-withdraw penalty.

Retiring Early with Retirement Accounts

Retire early with retirement accounts? That doesn't sound right. I can't take out the money until I'm already in retirement age! That's true, you can't make withdrawals from retirement accounts without the penalty. However, tax law gives us some work-arounds that allow us to withdraw early.

The Taxable Account

The taxable account? But that doesn't sound like a retirement account! This account is just your typical brokerage account, no retirement strings attached. This will help us during our early retirement years as a bridge until we can withdraw from retirement accounts.

The Strategy

This strategy will use a combination of the Traditional 401K, Roth IRA/401K, and a taxable account to achieve early retirement, while avoiding massive taxes both while working and during retirement. We will be using projections generated from my Investment Calculator to show how all the pieces work together.

Here are the steps:

  1. Contribute as much as possible to a Traditional 401K until our projections show that future RMDs would push us into a higher tax bracket than what we are paying now, or until we cannot afford to.
  2. Contribute into a taxable account the remainder of leftover income after expenses, until our projections show that this account will have enough to bridge into retirement years.
  3. Contribute the rest into a Roth 401K/IRA if possible.

So how do these steps make us retire early? This strategy doesn't look particularly special... Unless we utilize a little known technique called the Roth conversion. A Roth conversion is a transfer of funds from a Traditional 401K/IRA to a Roth IRA. A Roth conversion has no income limitions and no transfer limits. The one catch is that the amount transfered counts as taxable income, since we are converting pre-tax dollars to an after-tax account. We will utilize our taxable account during our Roth conversion years to maximize our conversions with little to no taxes. This is what allows us to stuff our 401K, saving taxes now, and then pull these funds out far before retirement years, paying no taxes again, and making future RMDs a beast that can be tamed.

Conclusion

An optimized strategy consisting of a 401K, Roth IRA, and Taxable accounts gives anywhere between zero to eight years of extra retirement. The average simulation gains an additional year compared to a strategy using only the Taxable account. Although there are retirement accounts in the United States that allow investors to invest with tax incentives, these incentives are not powerful enough to noticeably decrease the age one retires.
